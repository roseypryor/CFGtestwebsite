What is an assistance dog?

An assistance dog is a dog specifically trained to a high standard to mitigate a person's disability. A disability is defined by the Equality Act 2010 as ‘a physical or mental impairment which has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on the ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities’.

You may have heard of two of the most recognised types of assistance dogs; Guide Dogs, and Hearing Dogs. However, assistance dogs are also trained to help with many other disabilities such as physical mobility issues, epilepsy, diabetes, PTSD, and more.

You may be most familiar with the typically-seen harness or jacket. However, it is important to remember that the law does not require the dog to wear a these to identify it as an assistance dog.

How are assistance dogs trained?

The Equality Act 2010 does not specify that an assistance dog must be trained by a particular organisation. It is therefore possible to train you own dog to be an assistance dog, providing they meet the high standard required of them. However, training your own dog is something that is not accessible to everyone, due to the nature of disability.

With the aim of promoting the benefits of a standards-based approach to assistance dog training and welfare, the Assistance Dogs UK (ADUK) charity was set up in 1995. It is a voluntary coalition of assistance dog charities that have been accredited by Assistance Dogs International (ADI) and/or The International Guide Dog Federation (IGDF).
